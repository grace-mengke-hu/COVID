 Don't get too excited by the Oxford vaccine it is still very early days There's a lot of buzz over the Oxford vaccine, but this is not going to be a magic pill that we can take and get back to normal, not even if everyone is vaccinated.

In [the initial animal trials on rhesus monkeys]( the monkeys were vaccinated and then exposed to the Corona virus. Results were mixed.

* On the positive side, none of the vaccinated monkeys developed Covid-19 pneumonia, and their lungs appeared to be free of the virus.

* On the negative side, [they still got infected]( they had the virus in their upper repository track (the nose).

So we know that the Oxford vaccine doesn't give full immunity to the virus. It is, at *best*, a palliative to reduce the seriousness of the disease.

The monkeys were killed after seven days, so we don't know if the virus would have migrated to other parts of the body, including the heart and brain. It is quite possible that the vaccine will protect people from Covid-19 pneumonia while still leaving them vulnerable to [heart]( and [neurological damage](

So there is certainly some good effect of the vaccine, but **it does not make you immune to the Covid-19 corona virus**.

In human trials, obviously the trials don't intentionally infect human subjects with the virus. In [Stage One and Two trials]( a total of 1077 people were given the vaccine, or a control. (So only about 500 actually received the new vaccine.)

The good news:

* No serious side effects in any of the human patients. But with only 500 people receiving the vaccine, this doesn't prove much.

* Those who received the new vaccine did show an immune response, both an antibody response and a T cell response.

* At the end of the trial, 56 days, the immunity had gone down a bit but was still active.

But the bad news:

* While the researchers attempted to exclude those who had already been exposed to the virus, [some of the subjects turned out to have pre-existing immunity, suggesting]( that they were recovered asymptomatic cases with natural immunity. That complicates any judgement of how effective the vaccine might be: the vaccine may be less effective than the results suggest.

* As of a month ago, there are no figures available for how many of the experimental subjects have caught Covid-19, so we don't know how much, if any, benefit the vaccine gives in human subjects compared to the monkeys.

* The antibody response (antibody titers) was not that much better for those who received the Covid-19 vaccine than for those who received a control vaccine. This suggests that the benefit is probably only going to be minor, not full immunity.

It doesn't invalidate the whole thing, but it does mean we need to have a bit of caution and be less gung-ho than the British press has been. This is a promising lead, not a slam-dunk.

Now for the dangers:

It is unlikely that there will be major, serious and wide-spread side-effects, but there certainly could be risks for individuals.

On a national scale, one of the big dangers here is if the vaccine turns people who would otherwise get sick and stay home into asymptomatic spreaders. We know from the monkey trials that the vaccine doesn't give immunity so it is quite possible that people can be immunised, still get sick, and go out and give the disease to others -- even if everyone in the country has been immunised.

Since we know that a large proportion of recovered Covid-19 patients have serious long term effects such as neurological and heart damage, pushing this vaccine out could be an own-goal.

Up to this point I have tried to avoid being political, but I think it is important to recognise that some people, including some politicians, are looking for a magic pill that will allow them to re-open everything and let the economy go back to normal. For them the Oxford vaccine is that magic pill.

But because it doesn't give full immunity, if we re-open everything too early due to over-confidence, we could be dealing with tens or hundreds of thousands of asymptomatic Covid-19 cases after vaccination. Possibly as high as a quarter of them will suffer strokes and heart attacks, a major health disaster.

We have never before attempted to develop a vaccine in the short amount of time that we are rushing towards the Covid-19 vaccine. There is a huge amount of money at stake for medical companies that announce positive results and downplay negatives, to say nothing of national prestige. Russia has used their experience in developing two Ebola vaccines, which are described by the UN as experimental "potential vaccines", to develop the world's first Covid-19 vaccine. There's a catch though, or actually three catches:

- nobody really knows if it is safe: approval was granted after just Stage 2 trials, which involve only a few hundred people;
- nobody knows how effective it is;
- Russia is the country where a number of Covid-19 specialists "fell" out of second or third story hospital windows after going public with criticism of the government's handling of the epidemic, so there is a big question mark over the trustworthiness of any data coming out of Russia.

So the bottom line here is that the Russian vaccine is [a stunt]( and not something anyone should be taking seriously.

Let's hope that the Oxford vaccine turns out to be something better and we can resist the pressure to treat it as a magic pill that solves everything.
